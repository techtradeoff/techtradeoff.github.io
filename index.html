The Real Problems

Where senior professionals actually get stuck

These aren’t beginner mistakes. They’re structural gaps that slow down experienced engineers and architects — the problems without a Stack Overflow answer.

01 — Architecture Intelligence

You’re a Principal Architect. Your AI copilot thinks like a junior.

“You’re designing multi-tenant SaaS on Kubernetes. Namespace-per-tenant or cluster-per-tenant? You ask GPT. It gives you a balanced ‘it depends’ with no weighting for your constraints: 500 tenants, strict data residency, bursty workloads, 3-person platform team.”

Generic LLMs have no schema for enterprise tradeoffs. They don’t know when NOT to use microservices. They can’t reason about regulatory constraints, CAP theorem implications in your specific topology, or the 12 options you’ve already ruled out.

What you actually need: An AI that already has enterprise architecture patterns encoded — one that surfaces constraint conflicts and reasons about tradeoffs at principal-architect depth, not tutorial depth.
→ Addressed by archpilot
02 — Architecture Governance

Decisions made in the boardroom get unmade in the PR queue.

“Six weeks after your architecture review — where everyone agreed ‘no synchronous calls across bounded contexts’ — a developer opens a PR with a direct HTTP call from Order into Inventory. The reviewer doesn’t know the decision. It merges. You now have a distributed monolith.”

At 10 engineers pushing 20 PRs a week, you have 2–3 silent violations every week. Each one is invisible technical debt that compounds until it becomes a production incident or a full re-architecture project costing months.

What you actually need: Governance that lives where code lives — in the PR. Automated scoring that catches pattern violations before they merge, not six months later in a quarterly review.
→ Addressed by archpilot-reviewer
03 — Architecture Documentation

You have an architecture review in 3 days. The blank page isn’t moving.

“You need an ADR for the event streaming decision, a C4 Level 2 narrative, and a cross-cutting concerns document. You know what the right answer is — you just can’t turn it into a structured artifact that survives CTO scrutiny in under two days.”

A proper Architecture Decision Record requires structured problem statement, 3+ alternatives with pros/cons matrix, constraint mapping, decision rationale tied to business context. What AI produces without guidance is either too generic or requires 4 hours of prompting to correct.

What you actually need: A CLI that encodes enterprise-grade document structure so the scaffold is already correct. You add the judgment, not the boilerplate. Output that passes a review board, not just a spell-check.
→ Addressed by archpilot-cli
04 — Enterprise Presales

You built a $2M solution for a deal that couldn’t buy it.

“Three discovery calls. A detailed reference architecture. A CTO presentation. The deal is now stalled. Why? Nobody qualified the commercial risk. Procurement discovered the licensing model doesn’t fit their framework. Weeks of architecture work, dead.”

Technical presales is tribal knowledge. Senior SAs are great architects but poor deal orchestrators. Discovery goes deep on technical requirements and misses commercial risk, procurement constraints, and stakeholder misalignment. By the time architecture is done, the deal was never real.

What you actually need: A qualification framework scoring deals across architecture complexity, delivery risk, and commercial dimensions before you invest 40 hours in solutioning. The structured discovery-to-close workflow senior SAs have in their heads but never wrote down.
→ Addressed by cadex & presales-playbook
05 — Agentic AI in Production

Your AI demo worked perfectly. Your production system pages at 2am.

“Your RAG pipeline worked flawlessly in staging. In production: latency is 12 seconds, retrieval returns wrong documents 20% of the time, and when an agent makes a wrong decision, there’s no trace of why. On-call doesn’t know what to do when the agent loop gets stuck.”

80% of AI projects never reach production. Multi-agent orchestration diagrams look elegant; running them at scale is a different problem. You need evaluation pipelines measuring semantic correctness, distributed tracing across agent hops, fallback strategies when models degrade, and governance preventing irreversible actions.

What you actually need: A production strategy, not another architecture diagram. One that covers observability, evaluation, graceful degradation, and AI governance at the inference layer, not just at design time.
→ Addressed by AI/LLM Advisory
06 — Industrial Platform Engineering

Your team is reinventing an architecture that was solved 5 years ago.

“80,000 sensors publishing at 10Hz. That’s 800,000 events/second. Your team is still debating Kafka vs. Kinesis. Nobody has modelled what happens during edge outage: 2 hours of buffered data from 200 sites hitting simultaneously. You need 7 years of retention without a $3M/year storage bill.”

Industrial IoT at scale is a pattern-complete problem. Telemetry ingestion, edge preprocessing, time-series tiering, distributed coordination — all solved. Repeatedly, by different teams, with no shared reference. Every IIoT engagement starts from a blank Miro board and rediscovers the same failure modes in production.

What you actually need: A production-grade reference architecture encoding decisions already made at scale — so your team debates the constraints specific to your system, not re-derives known patterns.
→ Addressed by iiot-reference-architecture
